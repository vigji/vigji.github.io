{{< newthought >}}The most widely adopted{{< /newthought >}} firmware in the world (by number of installations) has an incredible codebase. Most of the code is completely ineffective, and it can be changed, deleted, and moved around at runtime. The whole thing has been repeatedly duplicated over the years, and whole segments consist basically of a few lines repeated over and over hundreds of times. Sometimes, the duplicated code has been tinkered with to develop new features; some others, the duplication of identical code just ensures that it is executed the correct number of times. There are portions of code that run only to copy-paste themselves around. Goes without saying, not a single docstring, or truly descriptive variable names.{{< sidenote >}}Even worse: analysts have been [piling](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9635429/) [up](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sonic_hedgehog_protein) [crazy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mothers_against_decapentaplegic) [nicknames](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LFNG) to talk about sections of the codebase.{{< /sidenote >}} And yet, its success is undeniable: it powers the most successful ecosystem. It constitutes the kernel of the only entities whose *agency* nobody would deny.

This is obviously the genetic code, where _code_ is to some degree a misnomer that would, if taken literally, encounter a frowned look from more than one biologist. Still, it is fair to recognise some very loose analogy with computer code. They both are durable registries whose changes, once enacted through the intermediation of an interpreter, translate into changes of a system and its ability to interact with the environment.{{< sidenote >}}This is pretty much as far as I will go in terms of analogy. The term "genetic code" as we know it was introduced by [in the 50s](https://profiles.nlm.nih.gov/spotlight/sc/feature/defining) and had more to do with the idea of a cypher to be solved. But before that, Schrödinger used "[code-script](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1369848616301042)" to mean a set of developmental rules — much closer to the meaning I am implying here.{{< /sidenote >}}

The way genomic evolution has produced the mind-boggling variety of life forms we know today sounds so miraculous that a significant fraction of humanity still struggles to believe it. DNA has just accumulated random mutations over millions, billions of years, and the differential reproductive success produced by the interactions between the organism and the environment has led to the adaptations and diversity we see today.

A central tenet of evolution is that it selects _at the surface_: when replicating, genomes accumulate mutations, so there is a constant source of **genetic variance**. Variants that produce phenotypes with a higher fitness in a given environment are more likely to propagate their genomes. Evolution does not care *how* a given trait is implemented, as long as it is expressed.

## The secret sauce of blind evolution: evolvability

I remember a passage from my high school biology textbook that likened the probability that a random mutation produces an adaptive change was compared to the chance that shooting at a car engine would produce an improvement in the motor. Taken at face value, such an image would destroy the conviction of even the strongest evolutionists. But it is actually an incredibly misleading mental picture: the car engine is a delicate engineered system where each component was designed, and its interactions with the rest of the system optimised, under the assumption reasonable users would not actually shoot at the hood to make the car go faster.

Genomes and organisms are a very different story. Since life exists and it has been self-replicating, adaptations to the environment could be driven only through DNA mutations. So even though each single mutation is not in itself adaptive, the system over time acquires features that make it more likely that further mutations actually are. Let's get more concrete. A major challenge of producing useful changes that make a protein acquire a new function, for example metabolising a new nutrient, is the fact that by changing it we would risk losing the function it had before. Here, genomic duplications are crucial. The _duplication_ of a whole segment of genome is not in itself adaptive. Even worse, it could produce imbalances that prove harmful for a cell. But it increases enormously the _surface_ for further adaptive mutations to happen: a second mutational event could introduce new features without breaking the old ones. Guess what, duplications are everywhere in genomes, and major innovation periods in evolutionary history have corresponded to duplication events that made available a lot of "tinkering material".

There is a long list of similar traits that increase evolvability by making the system more prone to benefit from further random mutations. There is _modularity_: the fact that while genes work together in the determination of phenotypic traits, they have limited, scoped competence and do not interact with every other part of the genome. The fact that organisms are made up of stacks of _semi-autonomous, hierarchical systems_ (cells, tissues, organis, systems...) is another feature that makes them more evolvable: genetic programs produce cells that are able to mind their own business without micromanagement from the organ they belong to, while still cooperating as a whole. In this way, the occasional reconfiguration of the high-level system could happen as the consequence of a single gene change — eg, *«the [Antennapedia mutation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antennapedia) in fruit flies, where a single regulatory change causes fully formed legs to grow where antennae should be, or the [loss of limbs in snakes](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5484524/) driven by degradation of a single enhancer sequence»* — can happen without specifying a whole different routine for each cell that composes it. But also the fact that they are _semi_-autonomous helps: the occasional message can travel up and down the hierarchy very easily, letting every cell in the body know our conscious mind is cogitating about food. _Robustness_ to mutation, the ability of not changing readily the phenotype after a mutation is, maybe paradoxically, also very useful. Once a mutation _does_ have an effect, that effect can be amplified by the previously accumulated neutral mutations.{{< sidenote >}}Mutations that do not have a measurable phenotypic effect.{{< /sidenote >}}

Evolvability solves the conundrum.{{< sidenote >}}For digging into evolvability, a friend guarantees me [this book](https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691134048/robustness-and-evolvability-in-living-systems) is great. *«See also [Kirschner & Gerhart, "The Plausibility of Life" (Yale UP, 2005)](https://yalebooks.yale.edu/book/9780300119770/the-plausibility-of-life/) for a more accessible treatment.»*{{< /sidenote >}}

## What about code?

I promised you code in the title, so, what about it? Well, turns out software engineers also talk about the evolution of their codebases.{{< sidenote >}}Maintainability is a more common term, but it is recognised that maintaining and evolving a codebase are two separate things.{{< /sidenote >}} People have observed evolutionary phenomena emerging in long-term code projects.{{< sidenote >}}*«[Lehman's Laws of Software Evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lehman%27s_laws_of_software_evolution), formulated from the 1970s onward, describe how software systems inevitably grow in complexity, must be continually adapted, and constitute self-regulating feedback systems — strikingly biological language.»*{{< /sidenote >}}{{< sidenote >}}*«There are papers directly comparing the organisation of genomes and software systems: [Yan et al. (PNAS, 2010)](https://www.pnas.org/content/107/20/9186.abstract) compared the E. coli regulatory network to the Linux kernel call graph; [Valverde & Solé (J. R. Soc. Interface, 2019)](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsif.2019.0845) analysed the Debian package dependency network as an evolving ecosystem.»*{{< /sidenote >}} So, what makes code evolvable?

Very interestingly, some of the evolvable features we saw above also appear as good coding practices. Code _coupling_{{< sidenote >}}Interdependencies that make the implementation of a single feature require a ripple of changes to be propagated throughout the codebase.{{< /sidenote >}} is the enemy. Therefore, modularity is a very sane principle: scope the pertinence of each component, keep its responsibilities narrow. Likewise, exposing stable interfaces between modules, behind which code can be changed without affecting how that module is used, is another core principle in software design; as is the layering of abstraction levels, each leaving the implementation details to the layers below. These map, to some degree, onto the principles we have described for organisms: modularity, hierarchy, segregation.

In software, however, those principles stem also from a necessity that is entirely absent in genomes: software has to be produced, fixed, maintained and evolved by human minds. Human tinkering is expensive, variance is not free in code evolution.{{< sidenote >}}We could obviously produce random mutations to code, and this is the principle behind genetic algorithms. However this has so far been unfeasible in classic software. *«The closest precedent might be [polymorphic and metamorphic viruses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymorphic_code) — malware that rewrites its own code with each replication to evade signature-based detection. A perverse but genuine case of code evolving under selection pressure.»*{{< /sidenote >}} Therefore, a clean codebase is the result of careful planning, skilled implementation, and thorough testing. Code variance comes from deliberate thinking and elbow grease.

## The era of vibecoding

I think that those intuitions might be challenged by **vibe coding**, a mode of programming where you "fully give in to the vibes"{{< sidenote >}}As per the [famous Karpathy tweet](https://x.com/karpathy/status/1886192184808149383). Vibe coding was Collins English Dictionary's Word of the Year for 2025.{{< /sidenote >}} or (in a sad attempt to make it sound a bit less improv theatre and a bit more LinkedIn skills), "agentic coding". In the most general definition, I use vibecoding as: declaring specifications that a program has to match, and fully delegating to an LLM agent (with tools to interact with a computer and the codebase) the whole code implementation. In the strict definition, you do not read the changes, and let the assistant push to your codebase anything that passes tests (either automatic or from your testing as user).

If you have tried it yourself or you have been reading something about it, you know the usual takeaways (as of Feb 2026): the technology is universally accepted as _incredible_ for short-horizon tasks and demos; as _maturing_ for saving human labour in producing production-grade code; and either _utterly useless_ or _revolutionary_ for working with long-horizon tasks with no human in the loop. The split is real, and the camps are very divided: some claim it is just leveraging technical debt to the sky{{< sidenote >}}*«[Technical debt](https://martinfowler.com/bliki/TechnicalDebt.html), a metaphor [coined by Ward Cunningham in 1992](http://c2.com/doc/oopsla92.html): shipping expedient but suboptimal code is like going into debt — it speeds delivery, but accrues "interest" in the form of slower future development and increasing defect rates.»*{{< /sidenote >}}, and cannot be used for production-grade code; most believe that it will completely disrupt the software industry in 2026. I think both can actually be true.

## Accidental vibe coder

In September I quit science and went to work as a data scientist. While waiting for the next data science commission, my company put me on an experimental project: implementing, deploying, and evolving features in a large ERP codebase with complex logic and interaction with several external services, vibecoded from scratch. There, I was _vibecoding_ in the purest sense: I could not read any of the codebase myself (the tech stack was Next.js/React with TypeScript and Chakra UI, and I only speak Python...).{{< sidenote >}}Tangentially related: it was fascinating to observe in such a clear way how much work is still there to be done for software when one does not write code. As a person with no training or experience in code production, I used to think a software is primarily defined by its codebase, and its environment is the machine it runs on. This is actually very wrong: it is just as much interaction within the world it is a part of and whose processes it models. And this is actually where even the best coding agents inevitably fail with their planning: they cannot see what's between the monitor and the chair.{{< /sidenote >}}

When working on this codebase, we used all the known drills. We set up our cursor rules, our AGENTS.md (in 2026, a markdown filename constitutes a standard...), declared all our nice codebase principles, employed state-of-the-art models. The codebase, once the project reached maturity (500k LOC{{< sidenote >}}Don't ask me, I'm a biologist.{{< /sidenote >}}, deployed since some months now), has become reasonably stable. And yet...

Yet the codebase keeps accumulating what an engineer would immediately recognise as code smell.{{< sidenote >}}*«[Code smell](https://martinfowler.com/bliki/CodeSmell.html) — a term [coined by Kent Beck](https://martinfowler.com/bliki/CodeSmell.html) — is a surface indication that usually corresponds to a deeper problem in the system: duplicated code, overly long methods, tangled dependencies.»*{{< /sidenote >}} Duplications are ubiquitous, and keep appearing. Code is basically never refactored, and carries around vestigial modules whose function is getting forgotten. And yet, the more we progress, the less interference new implementations have on the old ones.{{< sidenote >}}This is a very superficial gut feeling, and not the empirical validation of what follows. Feel free to be skeptical about this observation, and remain open about what follows!{{< /sidenote >}}

In light of all this, I actually started wondering whether it is possible that extensive vibecoding could set an "evolutionary pressure" that, as rounds of implementation and selection compound, _favours the spontaneous appearance of evolvable traits in the codebase_. Surely, each of those anti-patterns makes the code hard to read as hell (actually, nobody tries). But duplications, hierarchy violations, redundancies, reimplementations _do_ lead to uncoupling, which makes the code potentially more readily evolvable in the next iteration. To stress the point: this happens despite all the explicit prompts of "keeping code tidy" from the upfront `.rules.mdc` configurations.

To get more abstract: is it possible that there is an inescapable tension between *readable* and *evolvable*? Readability is a requirement that inevitably increases coupling: we want to reuse the same functions, adhere to our tidy abstractions, preserve layer segregations... And coupling is bad for evolvability: new features become inevitably harder to implement, bug fixes that were supposed to be small have to propagate changes throughout the codebase, etc etc.

Traditionally, the costs of losing readability were very clear in terms of increasing cognitive load and reduced productivity for developers; and duplications linearly scale the amount of labour required to fix bugs. But do the same constraints really apply when we can have free variance, and can afford to keep throwing spaghetti at the wall and see what sticks? Could it be better to maximise tinkerability at the expense of global analysability?

## Giving up control

I believe that software engineers will be increasingly pushed away (and upward) the chain of feedback loops that have to be closed for software to be maintained and evolved. As this happens, their understanding of the nitty-gritty will progressively lose relevance, and the definition of what "nitty-gritty" is will shift up the abstraction ladder. Issues will be automatically addressed. Sentry logs will directly feed agent pipelines. User requests could be automatically screened and lead to new feature implementations. Even further, users or groups of users could directly or indirectly (through their measured interactions with the software) steer the improvement of a tailored version of the application. In the backend, microservices offering the same interface with different flavours of internal logic could compete with each other and be selected on speed or robustness. Once free code variance is unleashed, it will lead to a Cambrian explosion in diversity of how software is produced. And this does not mean code will be super-humanly good, or performant, or crisp in its logic; quite the opposite, it could be made possible precisely by the fact that once we master _code evolution_, _code readability_ would become an afterthought.

Are we sure that in this massive reconfiguration we want to stick to the principles that until now have defined code quality in the eyes of the human developer? No matter how many trillions of human-typed tokens an LLM has ingested, a coding agent is a very different object from a human mind. _Jaggedness_, the idea that models are already super-human at many subtasks and sub-human for many others, is probably there for staying. And no matter how good they will get with code, it is unlikely that any granularity of planning will predict every possible interaction between software and the big world it faces in production. So why not loosen constraints, harvest variance? Abandon the idea of keeping code tidy by abundant upfront specifications, and just keep everything that increases fitness?{{< sidenote >}}We can obviously be strict and creative with what tests would be here: not just pre-release testing, but constant monitoring of performance and error logs, automatic A/B testing, resistance to any kind of intrusion or malicious inputs. All tricks that human coders are already using to define specifications, just turned into constant challenges for the codebase to evolve against.{{< /sidenote >}}

The tricks that evolutionary algorithms{{< sidenote >}}*«[Genetic algorithms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_algorithm), formalised by [John Holland in 1975](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Henry-Holland), solve problems by evolving a population of candidate solutions through iterative rounds of selection, crossover, and mutation — computationally mimicking natural selection.»*{{< /sidenote >}} have leveraged could actually become an inspiration for the whole software production chain. Why keep the bottleneck of a single software version released? We could start deploying whole pools of code forks, implementing new features on all of them, and screen the variants that have the best fitness in production — whatever measure of fitness we use. We can stop carefully monitoring each one of them, and just adversarially evolve all the fitness measures we use to drive their selection.

Humans have been engineering things without understanding for a very long time. *«We selectively bred [crops and livestock for millennia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Selective_breeding) before Mendel discovered the laws of heredity — maize was sculpted from a sparse grass over 9,000 years of blind selection. And now that we would know the principles of inheritance, mutating randomly genomes and »* But actively losing control of something we invented, something that powers a significant chunk of our society's information infrastructure right now, is a different kind of leap of faith — especially when the result, in the short term, looks worse than what our sweat and elbow grease could produce. But it already happened when artificial intelligence as a field moved from Good Old-Fashioned AI{{< sidenote >}}*«[GOFAI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GOFAI) — "Good Old-Fashioned AI," coined by philosopher John Haugeland in 1985 — refers to the classical approach based on hand-crafted symbolic rules and logical inference, as opposed to learning-based methods like neural networks.»*{{< /sidenote >}} to connectionism and modern neural networks. The trick was focusing on learning rules and hyperparameter tweaking, studying learning dynamics, optimising the boundary conditions that make optima discoverable — instead of micromanaging heuristics.
