On the other hand, I've always struggled a bit with the analogy. Not necessarily out of the whole "brain is not a computer" kind of vibe, which I do understand but only up to some degree. With all due respect to embedded cognition{{< sidenote >}}*«The family of theories known as [4E cognition](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/embodied-cognition/) — embodied, embedded, enacted, extended — challenges the view of cognition as purely brain-internal computation. So, what is wrong with the paper argument?

As I said, I do not think that the issue is some fundamental issue with the brain as a computer metaphor. I think the issue is more along these lines: all levels of a microprocessor have been engineered and optimised from the top down. While there has been a co-evolution of chip design and algorithms running on such chips, the system has been overall developed from the beginning in a very abstract manner. "Let's encode world entities in numbers - actually, binary numbers"; "Let's develop smart algorithms to handle logic and arithmetics on those numbers"; "Let's engineering a system that drive those binary representations around in the fastest way possible, with the shortest routes, and make them interact as much as possible"; Until, one day, "Let's wrap up all of this behind monitors and convenient user interfaces, so that people can literally move files and turn pages". The overarching result is something very compartmentalised in abstraction silos; whoever was working on the transistors engineering was hardly thinking about the nitty-gritties of GUI design or videogame logic; ditto for the converse operation. At the same time, multiple levels of this hierarchy were actively designed with this abstraction siloing in mind: it is generally a feature for operative systems to run on different kind of physical devices, and for program languages to be operative system-agnostic. This keep optimisation and design for each of those levels completely isolated from the others. Sometimes, things at one level need t change because of some interface changes at the level below, but most of the times changes are actually implemented while ensuring the same interface to the levels above.

This is very different from what happens in nervous systems. Neurons do not talk to each other, they do things to each other; the only way to change the behavior of the system as a whole is to tinker with how neurons wire, how they fire, how they interact with each other, *in a gradual fashon*. There is no way you can redesign a part of the system from scratch (say, visual sensory processing) and "expose the same interface". Just to be clear: learning is a very crucial part of the process. Actually, I don't like to cut sharp lines between development and learning. The nervous system is a hard-to-understand entity, which unfold over time during development in a constantly "self-organised", homeostatic ally tuned system that generates new rules and patterns as it expand. Its components have to constantly react to adapt to the chemical and electrical signals they receive, maintaining their own balance while listening to the ensemble messages that are providing feedback about the performance of the system as a whole, within its environment (being it an egg shell or a maze full of riddles). Any change to be implemented can never be catastrophically wrong; development and learning cannot afford to completely reorganise one part of the system in a disruptive way.

I think that one of the aspects that make neural networks so interesting is that their "learning process" impose the same constraints. The fitness landscape is explored gradually, with enough momentum to escape local minima but enough conservativeness to avoid the catastrophic loss of the gained performance.{{< sidenote >}}*«The phenomenon of [catastrophic forgetting](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1364661399012942) — where learning new information destroys previously learned representations — was first characterized by [McCloskey & Cohen (1989)](https://www.andywills.info/hbab/mccloskeycohen.pdf) and reviewed by [French, Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 1999](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10322466/).»*{{< /sidenote >}}

Actually, similar outcome came from inspecting the guts of image-processing convolutional neural networks, with activation patterns and processing strategies that could to some degree be probed.{{< sidenote >}}*«[Zeiler & Fergus, "Visualizing and Understanding Convolutional Networks", ECCV, 2014](https://arxiv.org/abs/1311.2901) pioneered deconvolution-based visualization of CNN layers. How come the epistemological toolkit of neuroscience was falling so short when facing a microprocessor, and look so effective when applied to these networks? I guess, and please tell me if the argument has been made more rigorously somewhere else, that the answer is in the gradual, continuous nature of the optimisation of the performance of those systems, that (starting from a specified structure, a scaffold of sort) then adjusted to mirror the relationships between the entities they operate on/with. As in brains, no epistemological siloing: the only way to steer the behavior of the whole network toward an improved performance is through an algorithm (backpropagation) that implicitly impute the network outcome to some of its elements and gently change them in a direction that makes the system as a whole better. I feel that there is something beautifully organic in this procedure, and probably tell us something about how biological (and neuronal) systems operate.{{< sidenote >}}Yes, I know, the brain does not do back propagation. Or does it? No come on, it does not. Anyway, it is still reasonable that the myriad of learning and homeostatic processes in brains achieve the same kind of smooth search of efficient "representations".{{< /sidenote >}} Even when the system evolves "emergent" algorithms, like the induction circuit, it seems to do it in ways that make it much more readable.{{< sidenote >}}We clearly understand much better a microprocessor than a LLM; remember that we are taking the stance of not knowing anything about a system, and trying to understand it just by watching it while it operates.{{< /sidenote >}}
