Some years ago, a famous paper([Could a Neuroscientist Understand a Microprocessor?](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1005268)) (mimicking an older and even more famous paper{{< sidenote >}}[Lazebnik, "Can a Biologist Fix a Radio?", Cancer Cell, 2002](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12242150/){{< /sidenote >}}) explored in a brilliant and only slightly humorous way the idea of if and how a neuroscientist could understand a microprocessor.

The paper looked at a microprocessor as a proxy for what a (simplified) brain does: taking some inputs (keyboard clicks), some notion of memory, an output (pixels on the screen). The authors used emulators available to run a full simulation of a simple microprocessor, which allowed them to put to work a pletora of techniques from the neuroscientists toolkit: they computed single cell tunings, performed "transistor ablations" (akin to brain areas/cell type lesions), analyzed local field potential-like signals, applied dimensionality reduction to population activity, and looked for correlations between transistor states and behavioral outputs.

As a neuroscientist, I have been two-minded about this take. On the one side, this resonates with the notion, which I hold dear, of the fundamental importance of [levels of understanding](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262514620/vision/) and epistemological coarse graining, and the piece is very thought-provoking in this regard. I do believe in the notion that the physical implementation of a computation can be to some degree isolated from the description of the computation in itself, which is one of the messages of the paper. 

On the other hand, I have been struggling with the analogy, this very direct comparison of microprocessors and brain.Not that I do not think that brains compute. I do not think that the issue is some fundamental issue with the brain as a computer metaphor.{{< sidenote >}}With all due respect to [embedded cognition](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/embodied-cognition/) and the alike, I do believe that the brain as an organ is highly specialised  for the aggregation, processing, and storage of information, under some loose definition of aggregation, processing, storage, and information.{{< /sidenote >}}. But the analogy feels a bit strawman-y, looking at the partial, incomplete, but very suggestive insights that neuroscience probing has obtained looking into brains. For modest that they might be, they do overshadow the paper pessimistic conclusions from analysing the microprocessor. We do have [Jennifer Aniston neurons](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature03687), we do have a long tradition of neurosurgeons evoking all sorts of profound phenomenological reactions by [probing the brain with micro currents]((https://academic.oup.com/brain/article-abstract/60/4/389/332082)). We can selectively [erase specific memory traces in the animal brain]((https://www.nature.com/articles/nature11028)), we can decode their future trajectories. So, what is wrong with the paper argument?

 I see more of what I would call an  "abstraction segregation" issue. All levels of a microprocessor operating have been engineered and optimised from the top down, and, for the most part, independently. While there has been to some degree a co-evolution of chip design and algorithms running on such chips, each layer of the system has been overall developed from the beginning in a very abstract manner. "Let's encode world entities in numbers - actually, binary numbers"; "Let's develop smart algorithms to handle logic and arithmetics on those numbers"; "Let's engineering a physical that drive those binary representations represented as voltages around in the fastest way possible to do math on them"; Until, one day, we got to "Let's wrap up it all in user interfaces, so that people can literally drag around files and see pages turning".

The overarching result is something very compartmentalised in abstraction silos; whoever was working on the transistors engineering was hardly thinking about the nitty-gritties of GUI design or videogame logic; ditto for the converse operation. At the same time, multiple levels of this hierarchy were actively designed with this abstraction siloing in mind: it is generally a useful feature for operative systems to run on different kind of physical devices, and for programming languages to be operative system-agnostic. This keep optimisation and design for each of those levels completely isolated from the others. Sometimes, things at one level need to change because of some interface changes at the level below, but most of the times changes are actually implemented while ensuring the same interface to the levels above.

This is very different from what happens in nervous systems. Neurons do not talk to each other, they do things to each other; the only way to change the behavior of the system as a whole is to tinker with how neurons wire, how they fire, how they interact with each other, _in a gradual fashon_. There is no way you can redesign a part of the system from scratch (say, visual sensory processing) and "expose the same interface". The nervous system is a hard-to-understand entity, which unfold over time during development and learning, while [generating new rules and patterns as it expand](http://selfassemblingbrain.com). Its components have to constantly react to adapt to the chemical and electrical signals they receive, maintaining their own homeostatic balance while listening to the ensemble messages that are providing feedback about the performance of the system as a whole, within its environment (being it an egg shell or a complicated maze). Any change to be implemented can never be catastrophically wrong; development and learning cannot afford to completely reorganise one part of the system in a disruptive way. Learning does not refactor.

It looks that somehow, this process ends up set in place a more tightened relationship between environmental variables and the internal state of the system. What in computers would sound utterly pointless (correlating the activity of sets of transistors to the "environment" or the "behavior") in brains become reasonable. You get your Jennifer Aniston neurons, your engrams, your face detection area, your mating-triggering optogenetic stimultion. Everything is obviously very complicated and escape reductionist thinking; but you can at least aim at finding a reasonable amount of mutual information between subsystems and the environment, much more than you would do with subcomponents of a processor.

## Neural networks as continuous learners

I think that one of the aspects that make neural networks so interesting is that their "learning" impose similar constraints. The fitness landscape is explored gradually, with enough momentum to escape local minima but enough conservativeness to avoid the [catastrophic loss](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1364661399012942) of the gained performance. The training process is continuous - in the matematical sense of proceeding without discontinuities. Even phase transitions in the loss curve come from the continuous adjustment of model parameters.

Language models are neural network that, after unsupervised training on text, can display unexpected and increasingly spooky capabilities in terms of generalisation, and maybe abstract reasoning, under some definition of reasoning. The perceived magic of language models comes also from the almost trivial simplicity of the optimised metric in their learning: they are simply trained on predicting the next word (token, in LLM lingo), given a number of words and sentences before (context window){{< sidenote >}}Not correct of course, this is just pretraining. Pardon the simplification here, the argument extend to post training as well{{< /sidenote >}}. Initially, the network spits out random garbage; then, it slowly realizes{{< sidenote >}}Please, bear with this and all the following anthropomorphizations. Whether an LLM really "realizes" or "understands" anything --_i.e._, if such terms are useful heuristics to understand LLM behavior - is for a different post.{{< /sidenote >}} that there are statistical regularities in the appearance of diades of words ("New" almost always precedes "York"; "ice" often precedes "cream"). After seeing enough of those diades, it start learning the abstract concept of induction: weights get configured so that if in the text a word A appears often before word B in the context, it gets more likely that the model predicts B following word A in the next appearance _even_ with words A and B that the model has never seen during the training{{< sidenote >}}Check [this source out](https://transformer-circuits.pub/2022/in-context-learning-and-induction-heads/index.html) for more on the magical - but improperly named - _in-context learning_.{{< /sidenote >}}. As they learn to efficiently predict the subsequent words, the models also evolve efficient _representations_ in how it embeds tokens in its internal vector space. They organize words (or concepts, to the extent a concept can be captured just by a word and its relationship with other words, without anchors to physical reality) in a multidimensional space that make operating on them convenient, and interestingly, from such space the relationships between words and concepts can be reconstructed, sometimes even with linear probes.

The whole field that is investigating this is called mechanistic interpretability. To me, this looks like LLM neuroscience, no more, no less.{{< sidenote >}}All of mechanistic interpretability looks like neuroscience conceptual and methodologial tools applied to artificial networks.{{< /sidenote >}} They look at unit tunings, "circuit" tracing, they crank up or down the activation of selected neurons, they ablate portions of the network. And guess what, they do see way more interpretable patterns than what you would get - and in fact do get - if you look into and poke a microprocessor! They can find many interpretable units (either directly or through specific lenses) predictably manipulate their activity in a way that steers the direction of the network; they did prove the bases for some induction algorithm, figured out how networks store [more features than they have neurons](https://transformer-circuits.pub/2022/toy_model/index.html) via overlapping directions ("superposition"), and [extracted millions of interpretable features](https://transformer-circuits.pub/2024/scaling-monosemanticity/) from production models. Most recently, [circuit tracing](https://transformer-circuits.pub/2025/attribution-graphs/methods.html) was used to map internal computational graphs of an LLM, revealing mechanisms for multi-step reasoning and language-independent representations. ["On the Biology of a Large Language Model"](https://transformer-circuits.pub/2025/attribution-graphs/biology.html) is a title that speaks for itself.

Actually, similar outcome came from inspecting the guts of image-processing convolutional neural networks, with activation patterns and processing strategies that could to some degree be probed.{{< sidenote >}}*«[Zeiler & Fergus, "Visualizing and Understanding Convolutional Networks", ECCV, 2014](https://arxiv.org/abs/1311.2901) pioneered deconvolution-based visualization of CNN layers. [Olah et al., "Feature Visualization", Distill, 2017](https://distill.pub/2017/feature-visualization/) made these internal representations strikingly legible, and [Olah et al., "The Building Blocks of Interpretability", Distill, 2018](https://distill.pub/2018/building-blocks/) connected them into interpretable circuits.»*{{< /sidenote >}}

How come the epistemological toolkit of neuroscience was falling so short when facing a microprocessor, and look so effective when applied to these networks? I guess, and please tell me if the argument has been made more rigorously somewhere else, that the answer is in the gradual, continuous nature of the optimisation of the performance of those overparameterized systems, which, starting from a scaffold of sort then adjusted to mirror the relationships between the entities they operate on/with. As in brains, no epistemological siloing: the only way to steer the behavior of the whole network toward an improved performance is through an algorithm (backpropagation) that implicitly impute the network outcome to some of its elements and gently nudge them to change in a direction that makes the system as a whole better. I feel that there is something beautifully organic in this procedure, and probably it tell us something about how biological (and neuronal) systems operate.{{< sidenote >}}And yet, if you talk about backpropagation to neuroscientists, they tend to get jumpy. Of course, the brain does not do back propagation! Or does it?{{< /sidenote >}} This overall smooth process resonates with the myriad of learning and homeostatic processes  that in brains achieve the same kind of smooth search for efficient "representations". Even when the system evolves "emergent" algorithms, like the induction circuit, it seems to do it in ways that make it much more readable when we try to figure out its function from direct "mutual information" measures between the inside and the outside of the system.{{< sidenote >}}We clearly understand much better a microprocessor than a LLM; remember that we are taking the stance of not knowing anything about a system, and trying to understand it just by watching it while it operates.{{< /sidenote >}}

## So, can they or can they not?

So, can a neuroscientist understand an LLM? Actually, can they understand a brain? I am afraid that once more, it is important to go back to the vexed question of what understanding means. If by understanding we mean finding internal variables ("representations") that track environmental and behavioral states and sequences, yes; if we mean being able to occasionally find empirically robust ways of steering those variables to change behavioral outcomes, definitively. But if we mean giving a throrough mechanistic view that accounts for how a given outcome is produced by some set of initial conditions with the same crisp resolution in which we understand how a key turn switches on an engine (or electrons are ultimately bounced through transistors as a result of our clicks), surely not.

No matter what our answer is, however, I believe that it would probably be the same for both brains and LLMs.
