{{< newthought >}}While foundational models'{{< /newthought >}} ability to deal with the truly unknown and truly novel is still largely unknown, It is by now very clear they are very skilled at replicating patterns. And since they are iper-parameterized, adaptable entities whose whole growth and learning process happens in virtual worlds, and for a large part on coding tasks, the patterns they [excel on](https://arxiv.org/html/2501.01257v2) are [codebases](https://www.swebench.com/) working with [onger and longer autonomy time spans](https://metr.org/blog/2025-03-19-measuring-ai-ability-to-complete-long-tasks/).


Models are incredibly good at pulling code out of thin air, and obstinately iterate until things work. But they regularily start to fail as the project scope increases; and while this is generally blamed on the models not being smart enough, I think that most of the times the issue is actually underspecification. As [discussed before](/blog/the-messiest-codebase/), when working with coding agents testing is everything. Indeed, if your specifications can be _very_ well formalised in terms of requirements to be matched and tests to be passed, you can consider your codebase aready implemented.{{< sidenote >}}Modulo some hundreds of dollars' worth of tokens.{{< /sidenote >}} 

I recently experienced this very strongly myself, as I used Claude Code to migrate a humongous Access codebase{{< sidenote >}}A company-wide ERP system built on 4 linked databases, containing 251 tables, 335 input screens and reports, and 37 custom code modules (around 10,000 lines of code). It handled operations for multiple subsidiaries across different countries, supporting several languages and currencies; and talked to external accounting and payroll software, featured FTP and emails integrations, and role-based access control and multi-tenant isolation.{{< /sidenote >}} into a more modern MySQL+PHP+Laravel web app, for compatibility with other existing software of the company. Goes without saying: I do not know anything of neither Visual Basic nor PHP nor Laravel. As a result, a work that was estimated in a couple of years of legacy development, or several months of AI-assisted coding was addressed in a matter of 3-4 days of uninterrupted agents runs, with some 3-4h of supervision per day. It was not trivial, it  required lots of tricks to organize agents to parse specifications from the old codebase and translate them in the new one without losing pieces along the way. We are definitively not at the point of a single button press for the whole thing to happen, but given where we were one year ago, it is safe to estimate that we will get there soon.

So, we are already at a point where generating a whole production-ready project is mostly a matter of orchestrating and spawning the right number of agents and allocating some budget for token consumption.{{< sidenote >}}My implementation cost about 2 weeks of Anthropic Teams premium seat, 6.25x the Pro.{{< /sidenote >}}, and we will probably get soon to the tokens being the only requirement. Soon enough, replicating a whole software stack will be something everyone could afford. I like to call this specific usage of coding agents for cloning software  _xerocoding_{{< sidenote >}}Or photocoding? Ironically, Xerox invented the GUI and the mouse, and famously lost it to Apple.{{< /sidenote >}}, to distinguish it from _vibecoding_ (where the direction and the objectives come from the user "vibes").

Xerocoding is popping up more and more in the news. Coding agents have been by now used to [rewrite entirely](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Mar/5/chardet/) complex python packages to change their license from GNU to MIT, and allow commercial usage, a practice that has stirred [quite](https://www.theregister.com/2026/03/06/ai_kills_software_licensing/) [some](https://www.osnews.com/story/144547/the-great-license-washing-has-begun/) debate; to [reimplement Next.js from scratch](https://blog.cloudflare.com/vinext/) using end-to-end testing. A [company](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Feb/7/software-factory/) that claims to be writing 100% of their code with AI <!-- (my pledge for 2026) — keep this? --> is describing how they could build "behavioral clones"{{< sidenote >}}The term "behavioral clone" comes from reinforcement learning — a policy trained to reproduce the input-output behavior of an expert demonstrator.{{< /sidenote >}} of existing software. In general, testing at the surface (as I was [discussing here](/blog/the-messiest-codebase/)) becomes trivially simple when the behavior of the codebase is already highly constrained by existing usable tests.

But in all this, what fascinates me the most is the point of what does software "amount to". The moment an agent can fill in the dots of detailed enough specs and testing requirements, those become the entirety of a codebase; writing code will be basically compiling requirements. Beyond the doomsday scenarios of xerocoding, there is a world where software will adjust to a whole new level of abstraction. Xerocoding will still leave open the space of how to deal with the code that still has to be written, or better: the specs and the tests that still have to be written.{{< sidenote >}}Pending word for how to call the minimal ensemble of specs that can produce a codebase with given requirements. Anticode? Code complement?{{< /sidenote >}} Massive code generation can't get too far in helping people untangling real-world data generation, organizational mess, or byzantine legal requirements{{< sidenote >}}[Jevons paradox](https://www.americanbar.org/groups/law_practice/resources/law-practice-magazine/2025/july-august-2025/jevons-paradox-and-the-legal-profession-when-efficiency-breeds-demand/): those just keep growing with the amount of time new tools liberate.{{< /sidenote >}} which will remain the true obstacles to be taken. But more and more this will be possible to address by people who have no familiarity with the nitty-gritty of code development. We will probably need new languages, both for specifications and for the codebase implementation.{{< sidenote >}}Contrary to what I thought, LLM proficiency with a language does not scale purely with training data availability. See [Grace Hopper's Revenge](https://www.thefuriousopposites.com/p/grace-hoppers-revenge).{{< /sidenote >}} A Cambrian explosion is around the corner.
